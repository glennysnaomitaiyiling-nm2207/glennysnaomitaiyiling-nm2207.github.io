4. Why is this an important question?

While income inequality in Singapore may not be considered one of the highest in the world, Singapore is ranked one of the most unequal countries among wealthy ones, second only to Hong Kong (according to Teo You Yenn, 2018) In 2016, the Gini coefficient in Singapore was 0.458, and the per capita income for the wealthiest 10% of households is $12773, 23 times more than the lowest 10% households ($543). There is also a the lack of a clear poverty line in Singapore, which makes it harder to determine who and how much of the country can be considered poor and requires additional assistance.

This, together with Singapore‚Äôs overall good economic performance, results in a situation where the ‚Äòpoor‚Äô in Singapore are obscured and, according to Nathan Peng (2019), there is a divergence between largely stable macro-trends and ‚Äúfelt‚Äù inequality experienced by citizens. While the state promotes ideas of meritocracy and equal opportunities, it is precisely these narratives that allow the issue of poverty to be seen as one resulting from individual flaws rather than structural ones (hence, the idea that poor people are simply lazy) (Smith, et al. 2015).

However, in reality there are factors which systematically disadvantage some parts of the population over others, such as intersections of poverty with race and gender.

According to the Census of Population 2010: Statistical Release 2 ‚Äì Households and Housing, the median income of Singaporean Malays, SGD 3,844 is well below the national average of SGD 5,000 SGD, and below that of Chinese and Indian Singaporeans, whose median incomes were SGD 7,432 and SGD 5,370 respectively. Other data also show how educational performance and sense of belonging are continuously tied to socio-economic position (Peng, 2019)
